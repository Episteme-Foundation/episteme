# Egg as food — nutrition and health effects

*Adapted from the "Nutrition and health" portions of the Wikipedia article "Egg
as food," available under the Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike 4.0 licence.
Lightly condensed. Included because it lays the conflicting meta-analyses side by
side — the clearest test in this cluster of whether the system represents a
genuinely unresolved empirical question as contested rather than picking a side.*

## Composition

A hard-boiled chicken egg (100 g) contains roughly 373 mg of cholesterol. In a
50-gram egg there are about 5 grams of fat, of which roughly 33% is saturated,
38% monounsaturated, and 20% polyunsaturated. Eggs are also one of the largest
dietary sources of phosphatidylcholine (lecithin) and choline.

## The dietary-cholesterol question

The article presents two standing positions. On one side, some research suggests
dietary cholesterol increases the ratio of total to HDL cholesterol and so
adversely affects the blood lipid profile. On the other, other studies show that
moderate egg consumption — up to one a day — does not appear to increase heart
disease risk in healthy people. The food scientist Harold McGee argues that the
cholesterol in the yolk is not the main problem, because saturated fat raises
blood cholesterol much more than dietary cholesterol does.

## Cardiovascular disease: conflicting meta-analyses

The epidemiological evidence is genuinely mixed, and reviews reach opposing
conclusions:

- One systematic review found that higher egg consumption (more than one egg per
  day) was associated with a significant *reduction* in risk of coronary artery
  disease.
- In 2020, two meta-analyses found that moderate egg consumption (up to one a day)
  is not associated with increased cardiovascular disease risk, and a 2020 umbrella
  review concluded the same for the general population.
- A 2013 meta-analysis found no association between egg consumption and heart
  disease or stroke.

Against these:

- Another systematic review reported that egg consumption was associated with
  *increased* all-cause and cardiovascular mortality.
- A separate 2013 meta-analysis found that eating four eggs per week increased
  cardiovascular disease risk by about 6%.
- Among people with type 2 diabetes, eating more than one egg daily was associated
  with a 1.69-fold increase in cardiovascular disease risk compared with eating
  less than one egg per week.

The article attributes these contrary results to differences in study selection
and to a reliance on observational studies, in which confounding factors are not
controlled. This methodological point — not any single statistic — is the heart
of the disagreement.

## A proposed mechanism, and a caveat

One hypothesized causal pathway runs through TMAO: dietary phosphatidylcholine
from eggs is metabolized by gut bacteria into trimethylamine-N-oxide (TMAO), a
compound linked in some research to increased heart disease. But another study
found that type 2 diabetes and kidney disease themselves raise TMAO levels,
suggesting the TMAO–cardiovascular link may partly reflect confounding or reverse
causation rather than a direct effect of eggs.

## Trial evidence

A 2018 meta-analysis of randomized trials found that egg consumption increased
total cholesterol, LDL-C, and HDL-C compared with no-egg diets, but not compared
with low-egg control diets — illustrating how much the measured effect depends on
the comparison chosen.
