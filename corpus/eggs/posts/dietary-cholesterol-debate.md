# Dietary cholesterol, guidelines, and the limits of the evidence

*A synthesis document on why the "are eggs healthy?" question has been so hard to
settle, drawing on the history of U.S. dietary guidance and the methodological
debate over nutritional epidemiology. Included as the cluster's epistemics
document: the FLF eggs prompt notes that "getting resolution on what are the
important things to answer and what are the appropriate ways of knowing is (more
than) half of the challenge." This document is mostly about ways of knowing.*

## The guideline reversal

For roughly forty years, U.S. dietary guidance advised limiting dietary
cholesterol to under 300 mg per day — a ceiling that effectively warned against
eating more than one egg a day. In the 2015–2020 Dietary Guidelines for
Americans, that numerical limit was dropped. The advisory committee concluded that
"cholesterol is not a nutrient of concern for overconsumption," reflecting
evidence that dietary cholesterol has a weak and variable effect on blood
cholesterol for most people. The guidelines still advised eating as little
dietary cholesterol as practical, and the reversal was itself contested — a
reminder that official guidance is a summary of contested evidence, not a fact.

## Why the studies disagree: observational evidence

Most of what we know about eggs and long-term heart disease comes from
observational cohort studies, which follow large groups and correlate
self-reported egg intake with later disease. These studies are powerful for
generating hypotheses but have structural weaknesses:

- **Confounding.** People who eat many eggs may differ systematically from those
  who don't — in smoking, exercise, overall diet, or socioeconomic status. A
  classic pattern is that, in some populations and eras, frequent egg eaters also
  ate more processed meat and fewer vegetables, so the egg "effect" may be the
  effect of an unhealthy dietary pattern.
- **Healthy-user and reverse-causation effects.** People warned about cholesterol
  (e.g., those already at cardiovascular risk) may cut eggs, making eggs look
  artificially safe — or the reverse.
- **Measurement error.** Dietary intake is usually captured by food-frequency
  questionnaires, which are noisy and often collected only once, years before the
  outcome.

Because of these issues, two competent teams can analyze overlapping data, make
different choices about which confounders to adjust for and which comparison to
use, and reach opposite conclusions — which is exactly what the egg
meta-analyses show.

## Why the trials are limited

Randomized controlled trials avoid confounding by assigning egg intake at random,
but the trials that exist mostly measure **biomarkers** (blood LDL, HDL, total
cholesterol) over weeks, not **hard outcomes** (heart attacks, deaths) over
decades. They consistently show that adding eggs raises LDL relative to a no-egg
diet — but raising a biomarker is not the same as causing disease, and the effect
shrinks or vanishes when eggs replace other foods rather than being added on top.
No long, large randomized trial has measured the effect of eggs on cardiovascular
events directly, and for ethical and practical reasons one probably never will.

## What actually seems to be the crux

The disagreement about eggs is less about any single number than about how to
weigh evidence:

- How much should we trust observational associations that survive adjustment for
  known confounders, given that unknown confounders may remain?
- Should we anchor on the biomarker trials (eggs raise LDL) or on the
  hard-outcome cohorts (mixed, mostly null for healthy people)?
- How much should the answer be conditioned on the person — diabetic vs.
  non-diabetic, baseline diet, what the egg replaces?
- And what is the right default under uncertainty for a cheap, nutrient-dense food
  with a small, contested risk signal?

These are questions about ways of knowing, and they are why a mundane food
remains genuinely unresolved.
