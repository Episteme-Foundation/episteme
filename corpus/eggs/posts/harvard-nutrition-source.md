# Eggs — The Nutrition Source

*Adapted from the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health "Nutrition Source"
feature on eggs. Faithful to the page's claims; included as the corpus's
mainstream, nuanced view — it lands eggs "in the middle" rather than declaring
them simply good or bad, which makes it a good test of whether the system
preserves conditional and population-specific claims instead of flattening them.*

## Dietary cholesterol vs. blood cholesterol

One large egg yolk contains about 200 mg of dietary cholesterol — more than most
other commonly eaten foods. For decades this drove a recommendation to limit
eggs. But dietary cholesterol and blood cholesterol are only weakly related: for
most people, the cholesterol eaten has a much smaller effect on blood cholesterol
than the amount of saturated and trans fat eaten. An egg's roughly 5 grams of fat
is mostly mono- and polyunsaturated, with relatively little saturated fat.

## Eggs and cardiovascular disease in healthy people

A large prospective study following nearly 40,000 men and over 80,000 women found
that up to one egg per day is not associated with increased heart disease risk in
healthy individuals. So for a generally healthy person, an egg a day appears to
be fine. A 2008 report from the Physicians' Health Study, however, suggested that
going well beyond one egg a day could be associated with increased risk of heart
failure later in life.

## A different picture in people with diabetes

The evidence diverges sharply for people with diabetes. In the Nurses' Health
Study and the Health Professionals Follow-up Study, heart disease risk was
increased among men and women with diabetes who ate one or more eggs a day. For
this population the page suggests limiting eggs — on the order of no more than
three yolks per week. This subgroup difference is central: "are eggs healthy?"
has different answers for different people.

## The whole meal matters more than the egg

Eggs are rarely eaten alone, and what accompanies them often matters more than the
egg itself. Scrambled eggs with salsa and a 100% whole-wheat English muffin is a
very different meal — for heart health — than scrambled eggs with cheese,
sausage, home fries, and white toast. Eggs are also a convenient, inexpensive,
nutrient-dense protein source, which matters when the realistic alternative is a
sugary pastry or processed meat.

## Bottom line

Eggs fall somewhere in the middle of the spectrum of food choices for heart
disease risk: better than red or processed meat, not as protective as plant
proteins like nuts and legumes, which are associated with lower cardiovascular and
overall mortality. For most healthy people, up to one egg a day is reasonable;
people with diabetes or existing heart disease should be more cautious; and in all
cases the rest of the plate, and what the egg is replacing, is what really counts.
