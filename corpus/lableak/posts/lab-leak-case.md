# The case for a research-related (lab leak) origin

*A synthesis of the strongest lab-leak arguments as presented by lab-leak
proponents in the Rootclaim debate (Saar Wilf's side), the DRASTIC research
collective, and reporting on the DEFUSE proposal and intelligence assessments.
Included as the deliberate counterweight to the zoonosis case document; both are
steelmanned. This document argues for a lab origin; it is not a neutral summary,
and it concerns the accidental-escape hypothesis, not deliberate engineering or a
bioweapon (for which there is no evidence).*

## The claim

SARS-CoV-2 entered the human population through a research-related accident in
Wuhan — most plausibly the escape of a bat coronavirus that was being collected,
cultured, or modified at the Wuhan Institute of Virology (WIV) — rather than
through a natural spillover at a market.

## Geographic coincidence

The pandemic began in Wuhan, home to the WIV, the largest collection of bat
coronaviruses in Asia and a center for exactly the kind of research most likely to
produce a SARS-like virus. Of all the cities in China — and all the rural
locations near the bat caves where these viruses circulate naturally — the
outbreak surfaced in the one city hosting the leading lab studying them. Proponents
argue this proximity is a strong piece of evidence that conventional zoonosis
struggles to match, since wildlife spillovers usually originate in rural areas far
from major virology institutes.

## Capability and intent: the DEFUSE proposal

In 2018 a consortium including the WIV, EcoHealth Alliance, and UNC's Ralph Baric
submitted the DEFUSE grant proposal to DARPA. It explicitly proposed inserting
furin cleavage sites at the S1/S2 junction of bat coronavirus spike proteins and
building chimeric viruses to test their ability to infect human cells. The
proposal was rejected — but it documents that the precise feature later found to
distinguish SARS-CoV-2 (a furin cleavage site) was something this exact group had
planned to engineer into this exact class of virus, roughly a year before the
pandemic. Proponents argue intent and capability of this specificity cannot be
dismissed as coincidence.

## The furin cleavage site

SARS-CoV-2's furin cleavage site is its most anomalous molecular feature. None of
the ~1,500 known sarbecoviruses — the relevant viral subgenus — possesses a
functional furin cleavage site, and the closest known relatives lack even
precursors to it. The 12-nucleotide insertion that creates the site uses CGG-CGG
to encode two arginines; CGG is the rarest arginine codon in this viral family but
is a common choice in laboratory gene synthesis. To proponents, an out-of-family
insertion that confers human transmissibility and is encoded in a "lab-typical"
way is the single hardest fact for a natural origin to explain.

## The missing intermediate host

For the major confirmed zoonoses (SARS-1, MERS), the intermediate animal host was
identified within months to a couple of years. For SARS-CoV-2, despite the testing
of tens of thousands of animals, no infected intermediate host has ever been
found. The environmental market samples show viral RNA co-located with animal
stalls but do not establish that an infected animal — rather than an infected
human vendor or shopper — deposited it. The absence of a found animal host, years
on, is offered as evidence against the market-spillover story.

## Ascertainment bias at the market

Proponents argue the market clustering is at least partly an artifact of where
authorities looked first. Once the market was identified early as a suspected
source, case-finding and environmental sampling concentrated there, which can
manufacture an apparent epidemiological center. They note that the market may have
been a genuine super-spreader amplification site for a virus introduced from
elsewhere, without being the site of the original spillover.

## Independent risk assessments

Several official bodies have judged a lab origin plausible or likely. The U.S.
Department of Energy and the FBI both assessed a lab-related origin as more likely
than not (at low and moderate confidence respectively); in 2025 the CIA stated a
lab origin was "more likely," at low confidence. A reported (unpublished) 2020
assessment by Germany's foreign-intelligence service put lab-leak probability at
80–90%. Proponents also point to the documented history of laboratory escapes —
including SARS-1 escaping Chinese labs more than once — as a base rate that
zoonosis advocates understate. They argue China's reluctance to share early case
data and lab records is consistent with something to hide, and that the strength of
the early scientific consensus *against* lab leak owed much to politics and to
scientists' conflicts of interest, not only to evidence.
