# The case for a natural (zoonotic) origin at the Huanan market

*A synthesis of the zoonosis argument as presented in the published market-origin
analyses (Worobey et al. and Pekar et al., Science 2022), the Rootclaim debate
(Peter Miller's side), and subsequent environmental-sampling work. Included as the
deliberate counterweight to the lab-leak case document; both are steelmanned so
the graph holds genuine disagreement. This document argues for zoonosis; it is not
a neutral summary.*

## The claim

SARS-CoV-2 entered humans through the wildlife trade, with the Huanan Seafood
Wholesale Market in Wuhan as the site of the initial spillover — most likely from
infected live mammals (such as raccoon dogs) sold in the market's wildlife
section, themselves infected upstream on farms.

## Spatial and epidemiological evidence

The earliest known cases cluster tightly on the Huanan market. About half of the
first 40 confirmed December-2019 cases had a direct market link, and a geographic
analysis of the earliest cases centers sharply on the market rather than on the
Wuhan Institute of Virology. Critically, even cases with *no* known market link
lived disproportionately near the market — the pattern expected if the market was
the origin, not merely a workplace cluster. The market was not a random location:
it had very high foot traffic and over a thousand vendors, poor ventilation, and
wet surfaces, all of which favor transmission; the zoonosis side calculates that
only a small fraction of Wuhan locations were as epidemiologically suitable.

## The wildlife connection

The market sold live wild mammals, including raccoon dogs, which are documented
susceptible hosts and coronavirus carriers. Environmental samples taken from the
market in early 2020 were positive for SARS-CoV-2 and were concentrated in the
southwest corner, the wildlife-selling section — and specifically at stalls that
had held susceptible animals. The detected viral RNA was closely related to the
viruses infecting the earliest human patients. (No live infected animals were
recovered, because the animals had been cleared before sampling — a gap the lab-
leak side emphasizes.)

## Two lineages, two spillovers

Two early viral lineages, A and B, differ by two nucleotides. Lineage B was about
twice as common in early cases and was tightly associated with the market;
lineage A appeared slightly later and in the surrounding neighborhoods. Molecular-
clock analyses (Pekar et al. 2021/2022; Pipes et al.) give high odds that the two
lineages reflect at least two separate introductions of an animal virus into
humans at the market within days of each other — a pattern naturally explained by
multiple infected animals, and hard to explain by a single laboratory escape.

## The furin cleavage site is naturally explicable

The furin cleavage site, the strongest molecular argument for engineering, has
naturalistic explanations. Similar insertions occur in other coronaviruses (e.g.,
HKU1) by natural recombination. The SARS-CoV-2 site (PRRAR) is *suboptimal* — not
the sequence a competent engineer would choose (labs typically use RRKR) — and the
virus later evolved away from it, suggesting it was a natural, imperfect starting
point rather than a designed one. The "unusual" CGG codons do occur naturally
(~3% of SARS-CoV-2's arginine codons), and an engineered insert should have
mutated out over years of human circulation, whereas the site was retained because
it boosts transmissibility.

## The lab lacked the necessary virus

To engineer SARS-CoV-2, the WIV would have needed a close progenitor. Its closest
published virus, RaTG13, is far too distant (~4% divergent — decades of evolution)
to be the backbone. The nearest known natural relative, BANAL-52, was not
discovered until after the pandemic, in Laos, and the probability that the WIV had
secretly obtained and retained such a virus is very low. The DEFUSE proposal,
moreover, specified that the gain-of-function work would be done at UNC, not the
WIV.

## The base-rate framing

Past novel-pathogen outbreaks (SARS-1, MERS, HIV, Ebola, the 2009 H1N1 flu) are
all now understood to have natural origins, despite early lab- or bioweapon-origin
conspiracy theories about several of them. The prior for zoonosis is therefore
high. Wuhan's role is less of a coincidence than it appears once its size and
status as a wildlife-trade hub are accounted for. Two Science papers in July 2022
concluded, from independent analyses, that the outbreak began at the Huanan market
and was unconnected to any laboratory.
