# The DEFUSE proposal and the case from laboratory capability (2018)

*A faithful representation of the documented lab-leak argument that centers on the
2018 "DEFUSE" grant proposal, submitted by EcoHealth Alliance with the Wuhan
Institute of Virology (WIV), the University of North Carolina, and other partners
to DARPA's PREEMPT program, together with the capability arguments built on it.
Added as a real, citable source on the lab-leak side so the WIV-capability and
furin-cleavage-site cruxes carry an independent instance rather than resting on
the debate write-up alone. It argues for the plausibility of a research-related
origin; it is one side of a contested question. There is a scientific consensus
against deliberate bioweapon engineering, and this document does not argue for
that.*

## The claim

The DEFUSE proposal shows that, eighteen months before the pandemic, researchers
at exactly the institution at the outbreak's location had proposed to build
viruses with precisely the features SARS-CoV-2 turned out to have. That
coincidence of capability, intent, and location is the strongest form of the
lab-leak argument: not proof, but a documented motive and means.

## What the proposal actually proposed

DARPA declined to fund DEFUSE, but the proposal was later released. It described
collecting SARS-related coronaviruses from bats across southern China and
Southeast Asia, assembling them, and testing their potential to infect humans. Two
elements are treated as especially significant by the lab-leak side. First, the
proposal discussed introducing "human-specific cleavage sites" into bat
coronavirus spike proteins to study how such changes affect infectivity —
directly relevant because a furin cleavage site is the very feature that makes
SARS-CoV-2 unusual among its close relatives. Second, the proposed work included
screening chimeric viruses in humanized mice and was to be conducted, in part, at
biosafety levels lower than many would consider appropriate for such work.

## The furin cleavage site

SARS-CoV-2 has a furin cleavage site at the S1/S2 boundary of its spike protein
that boosts transmissibility and that its closest known bat relatives lack. The
lab-leak argument holds that an inserted furin cleavage site is exactly what the
DEFUSE authors said they wanted to engineer, that no other sarbecovirus in the
same subgenus carries one, and that its presence is therefore evidence pointing
toward laboratory manipulation. Proponents note that the specific nucleotide
sequence encoding the site is a detail on which reasonable analysts disagree.

## Capability, access, and proximity

The WIV held one of the world's largest collections of bat coronaviruses and ran
an active program of coronavirus culture and modification. The outbreak began in
the same city. The lab-leak case treats the conjunction — a leading coronavirus
laboratory, a documented plan to engineer these exact features, and an outbreak
on its doorstep — as a coincidence large enough to demand explanation, and argues
that limited independent access to the WIV's records and databases has left the
possibility neither confirmed nor excluded.

## What this argument concedes

The argument does not assert that a recovered progenitor of SARS-CoV-2 has been
found in the WIV's published collection; its nearest published relative, RaTG13,
is too distant to be a direct backbone. It rests instead on capability and
proximity rather than on a documented intermediate, and it acknowledges that the
zoonosis side offers naturalistic explanations for each individual feature. Its
force is cumulative and circumstantial: the claim is that a research-related
origin remains a live hypothesis that the available evidence has not closed.
