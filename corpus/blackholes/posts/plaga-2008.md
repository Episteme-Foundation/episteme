# On the potential catastrophic risk from metastable quantum-black holes produced at particle colliders

*Rainer Plaga. Submitted to arXiv on 10 August 2008 (arXiv:0808.1415); last
revised 9 August 2009. This paper is the principal published dissent from the
LHC safety consensus — it does not claim the LHC is dangerous, but argues that
the safety arguments do not fully exclude a catastrophic scenario. What follows
is a faithful summary of its claims, included so the corpus contains the
strongest stated counter-position. Plaga's paper was not accepted by a
peer-reviewed journal, and Giddings and Mangano published a rebuttal
(arXiv:0808.4087) arguing that Plaga's central calculation is internally
inconsistent.*

## Abstract

Microscopic black holes that might be produced at the LHC are usually assumed to
evaporate harmlessly and essentially instantaneously via Hawking radiation. Using
a quantum-mechanical treatment, I consider a scenario in which a collider-produced
black hole is *metastable* rather than either perfectly stable or instantly
evaporating, and in which it accretes ambient matter at the Eddington limit
shortly after its production. In this scenario the Hawking radiation emitted
during accretion could be harmful to Earth and/or to CERN. I argue that such
black holes would remain undetectable in existing astrophysical observations, so
the astrophysical bounds invoked by Giddings & Mangano (2008) and Koch, Bleicher
& Stöcker (2009) do not exclude the relevant region of parameter space. I propose
feasible operational measures at colliders to reduce the remaining risk.

## The argument

The safety consensus rests on a disjunction: either micro black holes evaporate
almost instantly (the standard Hawking picture), or they are effectively stable,
in which case the white-dwarf and neutron-star bounds apply. This paper argues
that an intermediate case has been inadequately analysed: a black hole that is
metastable, evaporating slowly enough to persist and accrete, but not so stable
as to be captured and retained by dense stars in the way the astrophysical
bounds assume.

Two claims are central:

1. **The astrophysical bounds have a gap.** The white-dwarf and neutron-star
   arguments assume the dangerous black holes would be stopped and retained by
   those bodies. Plaga argues that in the metastable regime the relevant black
   holes would either not be retained, or would themselves evaporate before
   destroying the star, so the survival of white dwarfs and neutron stars does
   *not* constrain the metastable scenario. Existing astrophysical observations
   would therefore not have revealed these objects even if they exist.

2. **Hawking radiation itself is the hazard.** Rather than treating Hawking
   radiation purely as the mechanism that makes black holes safe, Plaga points
   out that a black hole accreting matter at the Eddington limit would emit
   Hawking radiation as a power source. The energy released during this phase
   could, on his estimate, be locally destructive.

Because the dangerous region is, by his argument, unconstrained by current
observation, Plaga concludes that the published risk analyses do not establish
that the residual probability of catastrophe is negligible. He recommends
precautionary operational measures.

## Status and rebuttal

In a follow-up note (arXiv:0808.4087, 29 August 2008), Steven Giddings and
Michelangelo Mangano responded that Plaga's scenario is internally inconsistent:
the same physics that would allow a black hole to accrete and radiate as Plaga
describes also forces it into the regime where their astrophysical bounds apply,
so the proposed "gap" does not exist. Casadio, Fabi and Harms (2009, published in
Physical Review D) separately argued that catastrophic growth does not occur in
the scenario Plaga considers. Plaga revised the paper in response to these
criticisms; as of those revisions it had not been published in a peer-reviewed
journal. It remains the most-cited stated dissent from the LHC black hole safety
consensus.
