# The safety of the LHC

*Statement assembled from CERN's public LHC safety materials and the conclusions
of the LHC Safety Assessment Group (LSAG). The LSAG review was published as
"Review of the safety of LHC collisions" (J. Phys. G: Nucl. Part. Phys. 35
(2008) 115004) and endorsed by CERN's Scientific Policy Committee.*

## The basic argument: nature has already run the experiment

The Large Hadron Collider can only reproduce phenomena that already occur
naturally. Cosmic rays — particles produced in outer space — collide with
particles in the Earth's atmosphere at energies far higher than those of the
LHC. Over the lifetime of the Earth, nature has already conducted the equivalent
of the entire LHC experimental programme about a hundred thousand times, and the
planet still exists. The Sun, the Moon, and other dense astronomical bodies have
likewise been bombarded by such cosmic rays for billions of years. Whatever the
LHC can produce, the universe has been producing all along without harmful
consequence.

Because of this, the LHC Safety Assessment Group concluded that **LHC collisions
present no danger and that there are no reasons for concern.** This conclusion
was reviewed and endorsed by a panel of external scientists and unanimously
approved by CERN's Scientific Policy Committee. The American Physical Society's
Division of Particles and Fields, the UK Institute of Physics, and the German
Physical Society have all endorsed the conclusion that there is nothing to fear
from particles created at the LHC.

## Microscopic black holes

According to the Standard Model of particle physics, the LHC's energies are far
too low to create black holes at all. Some speculative theories that posit extra
spatial dimensions suggest that microscopic black holes could be produced — at a
rate of perhaps one per second. Even in those theories, the black holes would be
harmless: they would disintegrate almost immediately through Hawking radiation.

Hawking radiation is thermal radiation that black holes are predicted to emit
because of quantum effects. The smaller a black hole is, the more strongly it
radiates, so a microscopic black hole would lose mass faster than it could gain
it and would evaporate essentially instantly. There is broad consensus among
physicists on the reality of Hawking radiation, but so far no experiment has had
the sensitivity required to find direct evidence for it.

Even on the extremely conservative assumption that some microscopic black holes
turned out to be stable, they would still pose no danger. A stable microscopic
black hole would be far too small — on the order of the Planck length — to
accrete ordinary matter at any meaningful rate. And the decisive argument is
again astrophysical: if collisions like those at the LHC could produce dangerous
stable black holes, then cosmic-ray collisions would already have produced them
throughout the universe. Such black holes, produced in collisions with dense
bodies like white dwarfs and neutron stars, would be brought to rest inside
them. The continued existence of white dwarfs and neutron stars — and of the
Earth itself — rules out the possibility that the LHC could produce a dangerous
black hole.

## Other hypothetical concerns

**Strangelets** are a hypothetical form of matter made of up, down, and strange
quarks. The fear is that a strangelet might convert ordinary matter it touches
into more strangelets in a runaway process. But the probability of producing a
strangelet *decreases* as collision energy increases, so the high-energy LHC is
even less likely to produce one than lower-energy machines that ran for years
without incident. Strangelets are only expected to be stable at low energies,
and a hot collision environment strongly disfavours forming a cold condensate —
about as likely as forming an ice cube in a furnace.

**Vacuum bubbles.** There is a speculative idea that the universe is not in its
most stable configuration and that a collision could tip a region into a more
stable "true vacuum." Cosmic rays have been colliding throughout the universe at
much higher energies for billions of years; if such a transition were possible,
it would already have been triggered.

**Magnetic monopoles** are hypothetical particles carrying a single magnetic
charge. If they are light enough to be produced at the LHC, cosmic rays striking
the Earth's atmosphere would already have produced them, and they cannot catalyse
proton decay in a way that threatens the Earth.

## Summary

Every hypothetical risk that has been raised about the LHC shares the same
refutation: nature has been performing the same collisions, at higher energies
and in vastly greater numbers, for billions of years, and the Earth, Sun, and
the densest objects in the cosmos are all still here. There is no basis for any
conceivable threat from the LHC.
