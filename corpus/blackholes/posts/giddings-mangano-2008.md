# Astrophysical implications of hypothetical stable TeV-scale black holes

*Steven B. Giddings and Michelangelo L. Mangano. Submitted to arXiv on 20 June
2008 (arXiv:0806.3381); published in Physical Review D 78, 035009 (2008). This is
the technical safety derivation on which the LHC Safety Assessment Group's
treatment of black holes draws most heavily. What follows is a faithful summary
of the paper's argument.*

## Abstract

We analyze the macroscopic effects of TeV-scale black holes that might be created
at the LHC, as predicted in some scenarios with large extra dimensions, under the
extremely conservative hypothesis that such black holes are stable and might
accrete matter. We extract the most stringent bounds by studying the effects of
black holes produced by cosmic rays striking much denser objects, in particular
white dwarfs and neutron stars. Their existence and observed properties place
strong constraints on the possible production and accretion of such black holes.
We conclude that there is no risk of any significance whatsoever from such black
holes.

## The argument

The starting point concedes far more to the worry than physics requires. Even if
microscopic black holes can be produced at the LHC — which requires speculative
extra-dimensional models — and even if, contrary to the strong theoretical
expectation, they do **not** evaporate via Hawking radiation but are instead
perfectly stable, we ask whether such an object could grow by accreting matter
and eventually endanger the Earth.

A black hole produced in an LHC collision and trapped inside the Earth would
accrete matter only very slowly, because of its tiny size. The relevant question
is the timescale: would it grow large enough to cause macroscopic damage within
the remaining lifetime of the Sun? We classify the extra-dimensional accretion
scenarios into two cases.

For scenarios in which a trapped black hole could in principle accrete on a
timescale shorter than the lifetime of the Sun, we show the scenario is already
excluded by astrophysics. The key observation is that cosmic rays produce exactly
the same black holes when they strike other bodies in the universe. A high-energy
cosmic ray hitting a white dwarf or a neutron star would create such black holes,
and because those bodies are vastly denser than the Earth, the black holes would
be captured and would begin to accrete. If these black holes could grow fast
enough to be dangerous, they would already have destroyed observed white dwarfs
and neutron stars on timescales far shorter than those objects' observed ages.

The persistence of white dwarfs and neutron stars — objects we observe in great
numbers and whose ages and properties we can measure — therefore directly
excludes the dangerous accretion scenarios. White dwarfs provide the binding
constraint for some regions of parameter space; neutron stars, being even denser,
extend the exclusion to the rest.

## Why the cosmic-ray comparison is decisive

A possible objection is that cosmic-ray black holes are produced moving at high
velocity and would pass through a body, whereas LHC black holes could be produced
nearly at rest and remain trapped. The paper addresses this directly: while
high-velocity production is true for ordinary matter, charged black holes and
black holes produced in certain configurations would be stopped inside white
dwarfs and neutron stars regardless. The enormous density and gravitational
binding of these bodies guarantees capture of a sufficient population to make the
argument work. Once captured, a cosmic-ray-produced black hole inside a neutron
star would accrete from a far denser medium than the Earth could ever provide, so
any dangerous growth rate would have manifested as the destruction of these stars
long ago.

## Conclusion

Combining the strong theoretical expectation that any micro black hole evaporates
essentially instantly via Hawking radiation with the empirical, model-independent
bound from the observed stability of white dwarfs and neutron stars, we find that
there is no risk of any significance whatsoever from black holes that might be
produced at the LHC. The astrophysical bound stands even if Hawking radiation is
assumed not to operate at all.
