## Background

Particle colliders are research instruments physicists use to understand
fundamental aspects of the universe. Because of the extreme energy levels
involved, public concerns have periodically emerged about whether such collisions
might trigger unforeseen harmful consequences. The Large Hadron Collider (LHC),
built by CERN near Geneva, began operations in 2008 as the world's largest and
most powerful particle accelerator. By 2015–2016, collisions reached 6.5 TeV per
proton.

Similar concerns had previously been raised about the Relativistic Heavy Ion
Collider (RHIC). Before RHIC started, critics postulated catastrophic scenarios
including black holes, transitions into a different quantum-mechanical vacuum
(false vacuum), or creation of strange matter more stable than ordinary matter.
A striking counterargument was that objects like the Moon have been bombarded by
cosmic particles of significantly higher energy than RHIC for billions of years
without harm. Scientists concluded that "beyond reasonable doubt, heavy-ion
experiments at RHIC will not endanger our planet" and that there is "powerful
empirical evidence against the possibility of dangerous strangelet production."
Physicists noted they cannot demonstrate a zero probability for catastrophic
events — they can only calculate upper limits. Martin Rees indicated the risk of
a RHIC doomsday scenario as being at most a 1 in 50 million chance.

## The LHC concerns

In the run-up to LHC commissioning, Walter L. Wagner (the original RHIC
opponent), Luis Sancho (a Spanish science writer), and Otto Rössler (a German
biochemist) expressed safety concerns and attempted to halt experiments through
petitions to U.S. and European courts. These opponents asserted that LHC
experiments could create low-velocity micro black holes that might grow in mass
or release dangerous radiation, leading to doomsday scenarios. Other claimed
potential risks included creation of strangelets, magnetic monopoles, and vacuum
bubbles. After his federal lawsuit was dismissed, Wagner told a Daily Show
correspondent that he believed the chance of the LHC destroying Earth to be 50%,
"since it will either happen or it won't."

The mainstream scientific response was emphatic. Brian Greene, writing in The New
York Times, reassured readers: "If a black hole is produced under Geneva, might
it swallow Switzerland and continue on a ravenous rampage until the Earth is
devoured? It's a reasonable question with a definite answer: no." The BBC
reported that "the scientific consensus appears to be on the side of CERN's
theorists," who say the LHC poses "no conceivable danger."

## Specific concerns

### Micro black holes

Although the Standard Model predicts that LHC energies are far too low to create
black holes, some extensions of the Standard Model posit extra spatial dimensions
in which it would be possible to create micro black holes at the LHC at a rate of
approximately one per second. According to standard calculations these are
harmless because they would quickly decay by Hawking radiation — thermal
radiation predicted to be emitted by black holes due to quantum effects. Smaller
black holes are predicted to be larger net emitters of radiation and to shrink
and dissipate almost instantly. The LHC Safety Assessment Group (LSAG) notes that
"there is broad consensus among physicists on the reality of Hawking radiation,
but so far no experiment has had the sensitivity required to find direct evidence
for it."

According to the LSAG, even if micro black holes were produced and were stable,
they would be unable to accrete matter dangerously because of their extremely
small size, on the order of the Planck length. Such black holes would also have
been produced by cosmic rays and stopped in neutron stars and white dwarfs; the
continued existence of these dense bodies rules out the possibility that the LHC
could produce a dangerous black hole. As the LSAG put it: "Black holes produced
in cosmic-ray collisions with bodies such as neutron stars and white dwarf stars
would be brought to rest. The continued existence of such dense bodies, as well
as the Earth, rules out the possibility of the LHC producing any dangerous black
holes."

### Strangelets

Strangelets are hypothetical small fragments of strange matter containing roughly
equal numbers of up, down, and strange quarks. If they exist and were produced at
the LHC, they could conceivably initiate a runaway process converting ordinary
matter to strange matter. However, the probability of strangelet creation
*decreases* at higher energies, so the LHC is less likely to produce them than
its lower-energy predecessors. Strangelets are bound only at low energies
(1–10 MeV), while LHC collisions release 7–14 TeV; the second law of
thermodynamics very strongly disfavours forming a cold condensate in a hot
medium — about as likely as producing an ice cube in a furnace.

## Concerns not meeting peer review

Otto Rössler argued that micro black holes created in the LHC could grow
exponentially. Hermann Nicolai, Director of the Albert Einstein Institute,
reviewed Rössler's paper and found logical inconsistencies and physical
misunderstandings, concluding that "this text would not pass the referee process
in a serious journal." Domenico Giulini and Nicolai concluded that Rössler's
"argument concerns only the General Theory of Relativity, and makes no logical
connection to LHC physics; the argument is not valid; the argument is not
self-consistent."

Rainer Plaga, a German astrophysicist, posted a paper concluding that LHC safety
studies have not definitely ruled out a catastrophic threat from microscopic
black holes, including possible danger from Hawking radiation emitted during
accretion. Giddings and Mangano responded, pointing to what they see as a basic
inconsistency in Plaga's calculation and arguing that their own conclusions
remain robust. Casadio, Fabi and Harms later published a paper ruling out the
catastrophic growth in the scenario Plaga considered. Plaga's paper has not been
published in a peer-reviewed journal.

## Safety reviews

Drawing on earlier RHIC safety work, the LHC Safety Study Group released the 2003
report "Study of Potentially Dangerous Events During Heavy-Ion Collisions at the
LHC," concluding there is "no basis for any conceivable threat." In 2007, CERN
mandated five particle physicists not involved in LHC experiments — the LHC
Safety Assessment Group (John Ellis, Gian Giudice, Michelangelo Mangano and Urs
Wiedemann of CERN, and Igor Tkachev) — to monitor the latest concerns. On 20 June
2008 the LSAG reaffirmed and extended its conclusions that "LHC collisions
present no danger and that there are no reasons for concern." The report was
reviewed and endorsed by an independent panel including Nobel laureate Gerard 't
Hooft, and unanimously approved by CERN's 20-member Scientific Policy Committee.
It was published in the Journal of Physics G. The American Physical Society's
Division of Particles and Fields stated that "this report explains why there is
nothing to fear from particles created at the LHC."

## Legal challenges

On 21 March 2008, Walter L. Wagner and Luis Sancho filed a complaint in the U.S.
District Court for Hawaii seeking an injunction to halt LHC startup. The U.S.
Government called the claimed hazards "overly speculative and not credible"; on
26 September 2008 the court dismissed the suit for lack of jurisdiction over the
LHC project, and a subsequent appeal was dismissed in 2010. A suit led by Otto
Rössler before the European Court of Human Rights was summarily rejected. In
February 2010 the German Federal Constitutional Court rejected an injunction
petition as unfounded, stating that opponents had failed to produce plausible
evidence for their theories.

A 2009 review by the lawyer Eric Johnson in the Tennessee Law Review highlighted
a structural epistemic problem: the scientific arguments are so complex that only
specialists can evaluate them, yet those same specialists are, by reason of their
career investment, inevitably biased toward continuing the experiments — raising
the question of who is positioned to assess the risk impartially.
